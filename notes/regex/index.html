MetaCharacters in Regex (Regular Expression)

Single Characters

  • \d = any digit from 0-9
  • . = any character
  • \w = A-Z or a-z or 0-9
  • \s = any white space
  • \W = anything that's NOT A-Z or a-z or 0-9
  • \S = anything that's NOT a white space

Single Character Examples

An example of a telephone number could be...

  • 123 4567 8910

...to write this in regex, you need to see that it is 3 digits, then a white space, then four digits, then another white space and then another four digits. You could write this a few ways:

  • \d\d\d\s\d\d\d\d\s\d\d\d\d
  • \w{3}\s\w{4}\s\w{4}

The second way will be explained in more detail later but basically, \w{3} means 3 of any A-Z or a-z or 0-9. These ways are specific ways but you can be more vague. Examples of this will be showed later.

Quantifiers

  • * = zero or more times
  • + = one or more times
  • ? = zero or one time (optional)
  • {min, max} = self explanitory
  • \w{5} = all 5 letter words

Quantifier Examples

If we take our phone number of 123 4567 8910 back again. We can express this by being vague aswell. Here is one way...

  • \d+ \d+ \d+

...this one says that we need a one or more digit followed by a space then followed by one or more digit then followed by one or more digit. You can see that we don't have to put \s to indicate a space, I just put a space in the regular expression.

If we had a number of phone numbers that varied in length then quantifiers would be very useful. If there was a huge paragraph consisting of text and phone numbers and you just wanted to highlight the phne numbers then you would do this...

"You can reach me by my home phone number of 123 4567 8910 or by my work phone number of 12345 678910."

You can see that the two phone numbers have different formats so you can highlight them both by writing this:

  • \d+ \d+ \d*

This works because we are asking for one or more digits followed by one or more digits followed by zero or more digits.

Positional Characters

  • ^ = beginning of a line
  • $ = end of a line
  • \b = word boundrary

Positional Character Examples

This example will show you how to highlight one word lines. If we had the peice of text...

  • Hello
  • my name is
  • Jess
  • how are
  • you

To find all one word lines, you could write ^\w+$ This would work because it says that at the start of the line that is one or more charcters and then the line ends.

Other Characters

  • [abc] = a or b or c
  • [-.] = a literal - or a literal .
  • \. = a literal .
  • \( = a literal (
  • [a-d] = a through d (a or b or c or d)
  • [^abc] = anything apart from a, b or c (NOT a or b or c)
  • (now|own) = now or own

Other Character Examples

In the sentence...

  • The lynk is a link don't you think?

...if you wanted to highlight the word lynk and the word link and nothing else, then you could do it this way:

  • l[yi]nk

This way you are getting an "l" followed by a "y or an "i" and then follwed by an "nk". If you wanted the word lynk or link then you could write this:

  • (lynk|link)

This way, you are getting the word "lynk" OR the word "link".
